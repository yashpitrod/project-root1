# Stress, Anxiety & Mental Health

## Symptoms
- Persistent worry or feeling of dread that is difficult to control
- Difficulty concentrating on studies, assignments, or exams
- Sleep disturbances: difficulty falling asleep, frequent waking, or oversleeping
- Physical symptoms of anxiety: rapid heartbeat, sweating, trembling, stomach upset
- Feeling overwhelmed by academic workload, deadlines, or social situations
- Irritability, mood swings, or emotional outbursts
- Muscle tension, headaches, or jaw clenching
- Fatigue and low energy despite adequate rest
- Social withdrawal and avoiding friends, classes, or activities
- Loss of interest in activities previously enjoyed (anhedonia)
- Changes in appetite: overeating or undereating

## Red Flags
- Suicidal thoughts, self-harm ideation, or expressing a desire to die — this is an emergency
- Panic attacks: sudden intense fear with chest pain, shortness of breath, dizziness, and feeling of impending doom
- Severe dissociation: feeling detached from reality, not recognizing surroundings
- Psychotic symptoms: hearing voices, paranoia, or severely disordered thinking
- Inability to perform basic daily functions (eating, sleeping, attending any classes) for more than 1 week
- Substance abuse as a coping mechanism (excessive alcohol, drugs)
- History of trauma with acute flashbacks or severe distress

## When to Escalate
- Any mention of suicidal thoughts or self-harm — refer immediately to campus counseling center or emergency services
- If the student is having panic attacks more than once a week
- If anxiety or depression is severe enough to prevent attending classes for more than 5 consecutive days
- If the student is using substances to cope
- If the student requests professional mental health support — facilitate referral promptly
- If there is a sudden personality change noticed by friends or faculty

## Self-Care
- Maintain a regular daily routine: consistent sleep, meals, and study schedule
- Practice deep breathing exercises: 4-7-8 technique (inhale 4s, hold 7s, exhale 8s)
- Physical exercise: even 20-30 minutes of walking daily significantly reduces anxiety
- Limit caffeine intake — caffeine can worsen anxiety symptoms
- Practice mindfulness or meditation using free apps (Headspace, Calm, Insight Timer)
- Talk to a trusted friend, family member, or campus peer counselor
- Break large tasks into smaller, manageable steps to reduce overwhelm
- Limit social media and news consumption if they increase stress
- Use campus counseling services — they are free and confidential for students
- Join study groups or social activities to combat isolation
- Journaling: write down worries and thoughts to process them externally
