# Back Pain

## Symptoms
- Dull, aching pain in the lower back (most common)
- Stiffness and reduced flexibility in the back
- Pain that worsens with prolonged sitting, standing, or bending
- Muscle spasms or tightness in the back muscles
- Pain radiating down one or both legs (sciatica)
- Pain that improves with rest and gentle movement
- Soreness after carrying heavy bags, improper posture, or sleeping on a bad mattress
- Pain triggered by sudden movement, lifting heavy objects, or twisting

## Red Flags
- Back pain following significant trauma (fall from height, sports collision, road accident)
- Loss of bladder or bowel control with back pain (cauda equina syndrome — surgical emergency)
- Numbness in the groin or inner thigh area (saddle anesthesia — emergency)
- Progressive weakness in one or both legs
- Severe pain that does not improve with rest and painkillers over 48 hours
- Back pain with fever and chills (possible spinal infection)
- Back pain with unexplained weight loss
- Pain that wakes the student from sleep consistently

## When to Escalate
- If there is any loss of bladder or bowel control — emergency referral
- If there is progressive leg weakness or numbness
- If back pain follows a significant traumatic injury
- If pain is accompanied by fever
- If pain persists beyond 2 weeks despite self-care measures
- If the student has a history of cancer and develops new back pain

## Self-Care
- Stay active with gentle movement — prolonged bed rest worsens back pain
- Apply ice for the first 48 hours (15-20 minutes every 2-3 hours), then switch to heat
- Take ibuprofen (400mg with food) for pain and inflammation
- Practice good posture: sit with back supported, feet flat on the floor, monitor at eye level
- Use a firm mattress; place a pillow under the knees when sleeping on your back
- Strengthen core muscles with gentle exercises: pelvic tilts, cat-cow stretches, partial sit-ups
- Avoid lifting heavy objects; when lifting, bend at the knees and keep the back straight
- Use a backpack with both straps rather than a single-strap bag; keep weight reasonable
- Stretch regularly if studying for long hours — stand and move every 30-45 minutes
- Yoga and swimming are excellent for chronic back pain management
