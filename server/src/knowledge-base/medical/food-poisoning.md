# Food Poisoning

## Symptoms
- Nausea and vomiting beginning 1-6 hours after eating contaminated food
- Watery diarrhea, often urgent and frequent
- Abdominal cramps and pain, usually diffuse
- Fever, typically low-grade (99-101°F)
- Weakness, fatigue, and body aches
- Loss of appetite
- Headache and dizziness from dehydration
- Multiple students affected after eating at the same place is a strong indicator

## Red Flags
- Blood or mucus in stool or vomit
- High fever above 102°F (39°C) lasting more than 24 hours
- Signs of severe dehydration: no urination for 8+ hours, very dry mouth, sunken eyes, rapid heartbeat
- Symptoms lasting beyond 3 days without improvement
- Inability to keep any fluids down for more than 6 hours
- Severe abdominal pain that is worsening rather than episodic
- Neurological symptoms: blurred vision, muscle weakness, tingling (possible botulism — rare but critical)
- History of eating raw or undercooked meat, fish, or eggs

## When to Escalate
- If the student shows signs of severe dehydration and cannot rehydrate orally
- If there is blood in stool or vomit
- If symptoms persist beyond 48 hours with no improvement
- If multiple students from the same hostel or mess report similar symptoms (potential outbreak — notify campus health administration)
- If the student has a compromised immune system

## Self-Care
- Stop eating solid food until vomiting subsides (usually 4-6 hours)
- Sip small amounts of ORS, clear broth, or diluted apple juice frequently
- Resume eating with the BRAT diet: bananas, rice, applesauce, toast
- Avoid dairy, caffeine, alcohol, and fatty foods for 48 hours after symptoms resolve
- Take anti-diarrheal medication (loperamide) only if there is no fever or blood in stool
- Rest as much as possible and avoid classes if symptoms are active
- Wash hands thoroughly and avoid sharing food or utensils
- Monitor urine color — pale yellow indicates adequate hydration
