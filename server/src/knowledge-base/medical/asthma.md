# Asthma & Respiratory Issues

## Symptoms
- Wheezing (whistling sound when breathing, especially during exhalation)
- Shortness of breath, difficulty taking a full breath
- Chest tightness or feeling of pressure on the chest
- Persistent dry cough, often worse at night or early morning
- Cough or wheeze triggered by exercise, cold air, dust, or pollen
- Difficulty speaking in full sentences during an episode
- Increased breathing rate and use of accessory muscles (neck and rib muscles) to breathe
- Symptoms that come and go, with symptom-free periods between episodes

## Red Flags
- Severe breathlessness: unable to speak in full sentences or complete words
- Blue discoloration of lips, fingernails, or face (cyanosis — medical emergency)
- No improvement after using rescue inhaler (2 puffs, wait 5 minutes, repeat once)
- Peak flow reading below 50% of personal best
- Confusion, drowsiness, or exhaustion during an asthma attack (respiratory failure risk)
- Silent chest: no wheezing in a student who was previously wheezing (paradoxically indicates worsening — airflow is too restricted to produce sound)
- Rapid breathing rate above 30 breaths per minute

## When to Escalate
- If the student does not respond to their rescue inhaler after 2 rounds of puffs
- If there is cyanosis, confusion, or exhaustion during an attack
- If the student does not have their rescue inhaler available during an attack
- If asthma attacks are occurring more than twice per week, suggesting poor control
- If the student is using their rescue inhaler more than 3 times per week
- If there is a history of previous ICU admission or intubation for asthma

## Self-Care
- Always carry a rescue inhaler (salbutamol/albuterol) and know how to use it properly
- Use inhaler with a spacer for better medication delivery
- Identify and avoid personal triggers: dust, pollen, mold, pet dander, cold air, exercise, smoke
- Take prescribed controller medications (inhaled corticosteroids) daily even when feeling well
- Create an asthma action plan with the doctor: green zone (good control), yellow zone (worsening), red zone (emergency)
- Monitor symptoms and peak flow readings regularly
- Warm up gradually before exercise; use rescue inhaler 15-20 minutes before exercise if prescribed
- Keep living space clean and dust-free; use dust-proof covers on pillows and mattress
- Avoid smoking and secondhand smoke exposure
- Get annual flu vaccination — respiratory infections can trigger severe asthma attacks
