# Sprains, Strains & Fractures

## Symptoms
- Pain at the injury site that worsens with movement or weight-bearing
- Swelling that develops within minutes to hours after injury
- Bruising or discoloration around the affected joint or area
- Limited range of motion in the affected joint
- Popping or snapping sensation at the time of injury
- Tenderness to touch over the injured area
- Instability or feeling that the joint may give way (ligament injury)
- Muscle spasms around the injury site

## Red Flags
- Visible deformity: bone appearing out of alignment, abnormal angle, or bone protruding through skin (open fracture — emergency)
- Inability to bear any weight on the affected limb
- Numbness, tingling, or loss of sensation below the injury (possible nerve damage)
- Pale or blue discoloration of fingers or toes below the injury (possible vascular compromise)
- Severe swelling that develops very rapidly (within minutes)
- Significant injury to the head, neck, or spine — do not move the student, stabilize and call emergency services
- Pain that is extreme and disproportionate to the apparent injury

## When to Escalate
- If there is any visible deformity or suspected fracture — refer for X-ray immediately
- If the student cannot bear weight at all on the affected limb
- If there is numbness, tingling, or color change below the injury
- If swelling does not improve after 48 hours of RICE protocol
- If the injury occurred during sports and there was a popping sound in the knee (possible ACL tear)
- If the injury is to the head, neck, or back

## Self-Care
- RICE protocol for the first 48-72 hours:
  - **R**est: Avoid using the injured area; use crutches if needed for lower limb injuries
  - **I**ce: Apply ice pack wrapped in cloth for 15-20 minutes every 2-3 hours
  - **C**ompression: Wrap with an elastic bandage (not too tight) to reduce swelling
  - **E**levation: Keep the injured area raised above heart level when possible
- Take ibuprofen (400mg with food) for pain and inflammation
- Do not apply heat in the first 48 hours — heat increases swelling
- Gentle range-of-motion exercises after 48-72 hours if pain allows
- Use a splint or brace for support if available
- Gradually return to activity only when pain-free during movement
