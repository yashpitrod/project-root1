# Fever & Flu

## Symptoms
- Body temperature above 100.4°F (38°C) measured orally
- Chills, shivering, and sweating episodes
- Body aches and muscle pain throughout limbs
- Fatigue and general weakness making daily activities difficult
- Headache, often dull and persistent
- Runny or stuffy nose with nasal congestion
- Sore throat with mild pain during swallowing
- Dry or productive cough
- Loss of appetite and mild nausea
- Watery or irritated eyes

## Red Flags
- Temperature above 104°F (40°C) that does not respond to antipyretics within 1 hour
- Confusion, disorientation, or difficulty staying awake
- Stiff neck combined with fever and sensitivity to light (possible meningitis)
- Difficulty breathing, shortness of breath, or chest pain
- Persistent vomiting preventing fluid intake for more than 6 hours
- Seizures or convulsions during fever episode
- Rash that does not blanch when pressed (petechial rash)
- Fever persisting beyond 5 days without improvement

## When to Escalate
- If temperature remains above 103°F despite taking paracetamol or ibuprofen
- If the student has an underlying condition such as asthma, diabetes, or immunosuppression
- If symptoms worsen suddenly after initial improvement (may indicate secondary infection)
- If there is severe dehydration with dry mouth, dark urine, or dizziness on standing
- If the student is unable to attend classes for more than 3 consecutive days

## Self-Care
- Rest in a cool, well-ventilated room and avoid strenuous activity
- Take paracetamol (500mg every 4-6 hours, max 4g/day) or ibuprofen (400mg every 6-8 hours with food) for fever and pain
- Drink plenty of fluids: water, oral rehydration solution (ORS), clear soups, and herbal tea
- Use a light blanket; avoid heavy bundling despite chills
- Gargle with warm salt water for sore throat relief
- Use steam inhalation for nasal congestion
- Monitor temperature every 4-6 hours and maintain a log
- Stay isolated from roommates to prevent spread if flu is suspected
