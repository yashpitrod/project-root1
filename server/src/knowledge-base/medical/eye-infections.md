# Eye Infections & Irritation

## Symptoms
- Redness in one or both eyes (conjunctival injection)
- Itchy eyes, especially in the inner corners
- Watery discharge or thick yellow-green discharge (bacterial conjunctivitis)
- Crusty eyelids upon waking, difficulty opening eyes in the morning
- Feeling of grittiness or foreign body sensation in the eye
- Swollen eyelids, puffy appearance
- Sensitivity to light (photophobia)
- Burning sensation in the eyes, especially after prolonged screen use
- Blurred vision that clears with blinking (discharge-related) vs. persistent blur

## Red Flags
- Sudden vision loss or significant vision change in one or both eyes
- Severe eye pain (not just irritation) — possible corneal ulcer, glaucoma, or uveitis
- Eye injury from chemical splash, foreign object penetration, or direct trauma
- Photophobia with eye pain (possible iritis or corneal abrasion)
- Hypopyon: visible white or yellow layer in the lower part of the iris (pus in the eye — emergency)
- Eye redness with severe headache and nausea (possible acute angle-closure glaucoma)
- Contact lens wearer with increasing redness, pain, and discharge (possible corneal ulcer)

## When to Escalate
- Any chemical exposure to the eye — irrigate immediately with water for 15-20 minutes, then refer urgently
- If there is a foreign body embedded in the eye — do not attempt removal, cover the eye and refer
- If vision is affected beyond what discharge clearing resolves
- If the student wears contact lenses and has a painful red eye — suspect corneal ulcer
- If symptoms do not improve after 3 days of self-care
- If there is significant swelling around the eye with fever (possible orbital cellulitis)

## Self-Care
- Wash hands frequently and avoid touching or rubbing the eyes
- Apply warm compresses for 5-10 minutes to reduce crusting and soothe irritation
- Clean discharge from eyelids with a clean, damp cloth from inner to outer corner
- For bacterial conjunctivitis: use over-the-counter antibiotic eye drops if available
- For allergic conjunctivitis: use antihistamine eye drops and cold compresses
- Remove contact lenses and switch to glasses until the infection clears completely
- Avoid sharing towels, pillows, or eye makeup
- Practice the 20-20-20 rule for screen-related eye strain: every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds
- Use artificial tears (preservative-free) for dry eye or eye strain relief
