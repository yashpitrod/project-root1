# Headache & Migraine

## Symptoms
- Dull, aching pain across the forehead, temples, or back of the head (tension headache)
- Throbbing or pulsating pain, usually on one side of the head (migraine)
- Sensitivity to light (photophobia) and sound (phonophobia) during migraine
- Nausea and sometimes vomiting with severe headache
- Visual disturbances before migraine onset: flashing lights, zigzag lines, blind spots (aura)
- Tightness or pressure sensation around the head like a band
- Pain worsening with physical activity or bending forward
- Headache triggered by stress, lack of sleep, skipped meals, or dehydration

## Red Flags
- Sudden, severe headache described as the worst headache of your life (thunderclap headache — possible subarachnoid hemorrhage)
- Headache with fever, stiff neck, and sensitivity to light (possible meningitis)
- Headache following a head injury or trauma, even if it seems minor
- Progressive headache that worsens over days or weeks
- Headache with confusion, slurred speech, weakness on one side, or vision loss (possible stroke)
- New headache pattern in someone who never gets headaches
- Headache with seizures or loss of consciousness
- Headache that wakes you from sleep repeatedly

## When to Escalate
- If the student describes thunderclap onset or worst-ever headache — treat as emergency
- If headache is accompanied by neurological symptoms (vision changes, weakness, speech difficulty)
- If headache follows head trauma within the past 48 hours
- If over-the-counter painkillers provide no relief after two doses
- If migraine episodes occur more than 4 times per month, affecting academic performance
- If the student has a history of hypertension and presents with severe headache

## Self-Care
- Rest in a quiet, dark room to reduce stimulation
- Apply a cold compress or ice pack to the forehead for 15-20 minutes
- Take paracetamol (500mg) or ibuprofen (400mg with food) at the first sign of headache
- Stay hydrated — dehydration is a common trigger on campus
- Practice relaxation techniques: deep breathing, progressive muscle relaxation
- Maintain a regular sleep schedule (7-8 hours, consistent timing)
- Avoid known triggers: excessive caffeine, alcohol, skipping meals, bright screens
- Keep a headache diary to identify patterns and triggers
- For migraines: lie down in a dark room, take medication early, apply pressure to temples
