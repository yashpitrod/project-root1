# Stomach & Abdominal Pain

## Symptoms
- Cramping or dull ache in the upper or lower abdomen
- Bloating and feeling of fullness after eating small amounts
- Burning sensation in the upper stomach area (epigastric pain, possible gastritis or acid reflux)
- Nausea with or without vomiting
- Loss of appetite and aversion to food
- Excessive gas and belching
- Pain that worsens after eating, especially spicy, oily, or acidic food
- Intermittent pain that comes and goes in waves (colicky pain)

## Red Flags
- Severe, sudden abdominal pain that is unbearable or causes the student to double over
- Rigid, board-like abdomen that is extremely tender to touch (possible peritonitis)
- Vomiting blood or material that looks like coffee grounds
- Black, tarry stools or visible blood in stool
- Abdominal pain with high fever above 102°F (39°C)
- Pain localized to the right lower abdomen with rebound tenderness (possible appendicitis)
- Abdominal pain with inability to pass gas or stool for more than 24 hours (possible obstruction)
- Severe pain radiating to the back (possible pancreatitis)
- Signs of severe dehydration: dry mouth, sunken eyes, very dark urine, dizziness

## When to Escalate
- If pain is localized to right lower quadrant — suspect appendicitis, refer urgently
- If there is any blood in vomit or stool
- If the student has not been able to keep fluids down for more than 8 hours
- If pain persists beyond 24 hours without improvement despite antacids and rest
- If the student has a known history of ulcers and symptoms are recurring
- If abdominal pain follows a recent trauma or injury to the abdomen

## Self-Care
- Eat small, bland meals: rice, toast, bananas, boiled potatoes
- Avoid spicy, oily, acidic, and processed foods
- Sip clear fluids frequently: water, ORS, clear soup, coconut water
- Take an antacid (aluminum/magnesium hydroxide) for burning sensation
- Apply a warm compress or heating pad to the abdomen for cramping
- Avoid lying down immediately after eating; stay upright for at least 30 minutes
- Avoid caffeine, alcohol, and carbonated drinks
- Practice stress management — exam stress commonly worsens stomach issues
- If bloating: try gentle walking and avoid gas-producing foods (beans, cabbage, onions)
