# Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)

## Symptoms
- Burning or stinging sensation during urination (dysuria)
- Frequent urge to urinate, even when little urine comes out
- Cloudy, dark, or strong-smelling urine
- Pelvic pain or pressure in the lower abdomen (more common in women)
- Mild low-grade fever
- Feeling of incomplete bladder emptying
- Blood in urine (hematuria) — may appear pink, red, or cola-colored
- General malaise and fatigue

## Red Flags
- High fever above 101°F (38.3°C) with flank pain or back pain on one side (possible kidney infection / pyelonephritis)
- Severe nausea and vomiting with urinary symptoms
- Chills and rigors (shaking) with UTI symptoms
- Blood clots in urine
- Symptoms in a male student (UTIs in males are less common and may indicate an underlying issue)
- Recurrent UTIs (3 or more in 12 months)
- UTI symptoms in a student with diabetes or immunosuppression

## When to Escalate
- If there is fever with back or flank pain — suspect kidney infection, needs antibiotics promptly
- If the student is male — UTI evaluation should be more thorough
- If symptoms do not improve after 2-3 days of increased fluids and self-care
- If there is visible blood in urine
- If the student has recurrent UTIs
- If the student is pregnant

## Self-Care
- Drink plenty of water — aim for at least 2-3 liters daily to flush bacteria from the urinary tract
- Urinate frequently and do not hold urine — empty bladder every 2-3 hours
- Cranberry juice or cranberry supplements may help prevent bacterial adhesion (evidence is moderate but generally safe)
- Avoid caffeine, alcohol, and spicy foods which can irritate the bladder
- Wipe front to back after using the bathroom
- Urinate soon after sexual activity
- Wear cotton underwear and avoid tight-fitting clothing
- A warm compress on the lower abdomen can ease pelvic discomfort
- Over-the-counter urinary pain relief (phenazopyridine) can reduce burning but does not treat the infection — seek proper evaluation for antibiotics
