# Insomnia & Sleep Disorders

## Symptoms
- Difficulty falling asleep despite feeling tired (taking more than 30 minutes)
- Waking up frequently during the night and having trouble going back to sleep
- Waking up too early and being unable to return to sleep
- Feeling unrefreshed or groggy after a full night's sleep
- Daytime sleepiness, fatigue, and low energy
- Difficulty concentrating on studies, reduced academic performance
- Irritability, mood disturbances, and increased anxiety
- Reliance on caffeine or energy drinks to stay awake during the day
- Physical symptoms: headaches, muscle tension, stomach upset from poor sleep

## Red Flags
- Loud snoring with observed pauses in breathing during sleep (possible obstructive sleep apnea)
- Falling asleep involuntarily during classes, conversations, or while driving (narcolepsy or severe sleep deprivation)
- Insomnia lasting more than 3 months that significantly impairs daily functioning
- Using sleep aids (OTC or prescription) daily for more than 2 weeks
- Insomnia accompanied by severe depression, anxiety, or suicidal thoughts
- Sleepwalking, sleep terrors, or acting out dreams (possible parasomnia)
- Insomnia caused by chronic pain or another medical condition

## When to Escalate
- If insomnia persists for more than 4 weeks despite sleep hygiene improvements
- If there are signs of sleep apnea (snoring, breathing pauses, excessive daytime sleepiness)
- If the student is using substances (alcohol, drugs, excessive caffeine) to cope with sleep issues
- If insomnia is co-occurring with depression or severe anxiety
- If daytime sleepiness is causing safety concerns (falling asleep in dangerous situations)

## Self-Care
- Maintain a consistent sleep schedule: go to bed and wake up at the same time every day, including weekends
- Create a sleep-friendly environment: dark, cool (65-68°F), quiet room; use earplugs or white noise if in a noisy hostel
- Avoid screens (phone, laptop) for at least 30-60 minutes before bed — blue light suppresses melatonin
- Limit caffeine after 2 PM and avoid energy drinks in the evening
- Avoid heavy meals, alcohol, and intense exercise within 2-3 hours of bedtime
- Develop a bedtime routine: light reading, stretching, deep breathing, or meditation
- If unable to fall asleep after 20 minutes, get up and do a calm activity (reading, not screens) until sleepy
- Limit daytime naps to 20-30 minutes before 3 PM
- Regular physical exercise (morning or afternoon) promotes deeper sleep
- Avoid using the bed for studying or watching videos — associate the bed with sleep only
