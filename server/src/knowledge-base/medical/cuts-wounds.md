# Cuts, Wounds & Minor Injuries

## Symptoms
- Open break in the skin with visible bleeding
- Pain at the wound site, proportional to wound depth
- Redness and mild swelling around the wound edges
- Abrasion (scrape) with raw, oozing skin surface
- Puncture wound from sharp object (nail, glass, thorn)
- Laceration with jagged or clean wound edges

## Red Flags
- Deep wound with visible fat, muscle, or bone
- Wound that will not stop bleeding after 15 minutes of firm direct pressure
- Wounds on the face, over joints, or near the eyes
- Gaping wound edges that cannot be held together (likely needs stitches)
- Puncture wound from rusty metal or animal bite (tetanus and infection risk)
- Signs of wound infection developing 1-3 days later: increasing redness, warmth, swelling, pus, red streaks, or fever
- Numbness or inability to move fingers or toes below the wound (possible nerve or tendon damage)
- Embedded foreign object (glass, metal) that cannot be easily removed

## When to Escalate
- If bleeding cannot be controlled with direct pressure
- If the wound is deep, gaping, or longer than half an inch
- If there is a foreign body embedded in the wound
- If the wound is from an animal bite (needs antibiotics and possible rabies evaluation)
- If tetanus vaccination is not up to date (especially for puncture wounds or dirty wounds)
- If signs of infection develop after initial wound care

## Self-Care
- Wash hands before treating the wound
- Clean the wound gently with running clean water for 5-10 minutes; remove dirt and debris
- Apply gentle pressure with a clean cloth or gauze to stop bleeding (maintain pressure for 10-15 minutes without peeking)
- Apply an antiseptic (povidone-iodine or chlorhexidine) to the wound
- Cover with a sterile adhesive bandage or gauze secured with medical tape
- Change the dressing daily or when it gets wet or dirty
- Apply antibiotic ointment (like Neosporin) to prevent infection
- For abrasions: keep the wound moist with ointment and covered to promote healing
- Watch for signs of infection over the next 3-5 days
- Do not use hydrogen peroxide or alcohol directly on open wounds — they damage healing tissue
